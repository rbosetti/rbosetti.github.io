Treatment Approaches

I have extensive training and expertise in a variety of evidence-based therapeutic approaches. When creating a treatment plan, I will draw from the approaches that are most relevant to your circumstances and needs. My expertise includes, but is not limited to:

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

CBT is a therapeutic approach that focuses on changing patterns of thinking and behavior associated with distress. CBT consists of:

  • Cognitive therapy strategies focused on identifying and changing thought patterns that are causing and maintaining problematic emotions and behaviors.

  • Behavior therapy strategies such as:

    • Exposure: systematically and collaboratively approaching avoided emotions and situations in a step by step manner.
    • Communication and assertiveness skills
    • Problem solving
    • Relaxation training
    • Behavioral activation: engaging in more activities to enhance mood and sense of control

CBT is supported by research and considered to be the “gold standard” treatment for a wide variety of problems including obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), panic disorder, social anxiety, generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), specific phobias, depression and bipolar disorder, attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), family/relationship problems, work/school difficulties, stress management, procrastination, sleep difficulties, and more. CBT is a time-limited treatment, typically lasting months rather than years. Clients learn skills that they practice during session and at home. The skills learned in CBT last and continue to help long after treatment is over.

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)

DBT is a therapeutic approach that was originally developed by Marsha Linehan, PhD, to treat chronically suicidal individuals diagnosed with borderline personality disorder (BPD), but has since been recognized as effective in helping individuals with a wide variety of problems, including depression and bipolar disorder, eating disorders, and negative patterns in relationships. A full standard DBT program is considered to be a higher level of care than standard outpatient therapy and consists of both individual and group therapy, as well as 24⁄7 phone coaching support. It is most appropriate for individuals who are currently experiencing active suicidal thoughts or engaging in self-injurious behaviors. While I have prior training and expertise in the full DBT model, please note that Compass CBT is not a traditional DBT program and I do not provide group skills training or 24⁄7 phone coaching support. I do provide DBT-informed therapy and teach skills from the following four DBT domains:

  • Mindfulness: Learn to non-judgmentally observe, describe and abide with your present moment experience in order to effectively respond to the situation at hand.

  • Interpersonal Effectiveness: Understand how to set boundaries and clearly express your needs.

  • Distress Tolerance: Learn how to get through very painful moments without making things worse.

  • Emotion Regulation: Learn to understand and reduce the intensity of negative emotions and increase positive emotions.

Mindful Self-Compassion (MSC)

MSC is a therapeutic approach that combines the skills of mindfulness and self-compassion and provides a powerful tool for emotional resilience. While everyone recognizes that compassion toward others is worthwhile and important, too few of us recognize the fundamental role that self-compassion plays in happiness, health, and relationships. We live in a society that encourages us to beat ourselves up in order to get ahead. Self-criticism is not effective long-term, and relying on external sources of validation only leads us to feel more anxious, insecure, and disconnected from ourselves and others. Over a thousand research studies have demonstrated that people who are more self-compassionate experience less depression, anxiety, stress, and shame and more happiness, life satisfaction, self-confidence, and physical health. Together, we can work on increasing your ability to relate to yourself with the same kindness and compassion that you would show to a good friend.

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

ACT is a therapeutic approach that uses acceptance and mindfulness strategies together with behavior change strategies in order to reduce suffering, create meaning, and honor values. ACT techniques can help you learn how to get unstuck from unhelpful thoughts, get in contact with the present moment, and connect with and commit to acting in-line with your values. The end-goal of ACT is psychological flexibility: being present, open, and doing what matters to you. ACT has been shown to be effective for a variety of problems, including anxiety, depression, chronic pain, and eating disorders.

Health at Every Size® (HAES)

HAES is a framework that supports people of all sizes in addressing health directly by adopting healthy behaviors. It is an inclusive movement that supports body diversity, recognizing that our characteristics, such as our size, race, national origin, sexuality, gender, disability status, and other attributes, are assets. HAES acknowledges and challenges the structural and systemic forces that impinge on living well.

Like so many of us, you may be struggling with negative thoughts about your body that lead you to base your worth on what your body looks like, obsess about your weight, and constantly compare yourself to others. You may even find that your relationships and ability to enjoy life are suffering. This is not your fault. You learned and internalized messages about what your body should look like. The good news is that you can learn to critically challenge and shift your view of your body. Whether you have been struggling with negative body image your whole life or a life transition, physical illness, or injury has sent you into a negative body image spiral, I can help you cultivate a sense of self-worth and confidence and live with greater freedom and peace inside your body.

Intuitive Eating

Intuitive eating is a weight-neutral, evidence-based approach for food and body image concerns. This approach was created by two dietitians, Evelyn Tribole, MS, RDN and Elyse Resch, MS, RDN. Intuitive eating comprises ten principles for nourishing a healthy relationship with food. With the help of intuitive eating, you will learn the skill of interoceptive awareness. You will learn how to reconnect to your body’s direct messages of hunger, fullness, and satiety so that you can better meet your physical and psychological needs and stop feeling guilty about how you eat.
